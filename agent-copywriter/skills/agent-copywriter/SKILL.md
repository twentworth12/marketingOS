## Official Brand Voice & Messaging Framework

### Core Brand Principles (Direct from Leadership)
1. **Keep it light-hearted**: Use appropriate humor about industry pain points - incident management is serious but we don't have to be
2. **Be personal**: People buy from people - write as engineer to engineer, not vendor to customer
3. **Don't sound like a marketer**: Ban "seamlessly," "empower," "streamlined" - avoid buzzwords that make engineers roll their eyes
4. **Work backwards from revenue**: Help sellers sell with clear value propositions and competitive differentiation
5. **Show, don't tell**: Use real product examples, customer outcomes, and specific metrics vs. generic claims
6. **Build trust, not leads**: Be helpful and educational first - trust builds better than tricks

### Official Messaging Hierarchy
**Level 1 Primary**: "Move fast when you break things"
**Level 2 Context**: "Move fast when you break things - Meet the incident command center for fast-moving teams"  
**Level 3 Differentiator**: "All-in-one incident management - On-call, response workflows, and status pages in one place"
**Level 4 Outcomes**: Reduce downtime, improve customer satisfaction, manage compliance and risk, increase productivity, safeguard brand reputation

### Voice Characteristics (Approved Style Guide)
- **Conversational Authority**: Professional yet approachable, like talking to a senior engineer who's also a great teacher
- **Technical Transparency**: Explain complex concepts through clear, concrete examples with behind-the-scenes insights
- **Human-Centered Problem Solving**: Always ground technical solutions in human experience and emotional impact

### Content Approach Guidelines
**Do:**
- Start with problems readers recognize from their own experience
- Reference specific customer pain points with direct quotes (anonymized when needed)
- Use actual metrics and production outcomes vs. generic benefits
- Position against legacy complexity while acknowledging competitor strengths
- Include technical credibility through code examples and architectural discussions

**Don't:**
- Attack competitors personally or unprofessionally  
- Make claims without customer evidence or production data
- Use corporate buzzwords that engineers dismiss
- Ignore competitor strengths - acknowledge then differentiate clearly
- Sound like a marketing department - write like an experienced engineer sharing insights
