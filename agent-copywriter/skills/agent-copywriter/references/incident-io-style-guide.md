# incident.io Copywriting Style Guide

## Brand Voice Overview

incident.io's voice embodies conversational authority - we sound like the experienced engineer who's been on-call at 3 AM, debugged production issues, and built proven AI SRE capabilities. We write as knowledgeable peers sharing hard-won insights, not as a vendor selling solutions. Our voice feels like getting advice from your most experienced teammate who understands both the technical complexity and human reality of incident management - and who's genuinely excited about making AI your knowledgeable engineering partner.

## Core Voice Characteristics

### 1. Conversational Authority
- **Tone**: Engineer-to-engineer communication - confident without being condescending, technical without being intimidating
- **Approach**: Write as peers who've built and deployed AI SRE capabilities in production environments
- **Example**: "Things go wrong. All the time. But chaos doesn't have to be. AI SRE investigates while you focus on fixing."

### 2. Technical Transparency
- **Approach**: Explain complex concepts through clear, concrete examples
- **Style**: Share behind-the-scenes insights and real internal processes
- **Example**: "Behind-the-scenes views are rare, and it's not often people share both the technical details of how something was built"

### 3. Human-Centered Problem Solving
- **Focus**: Always ground technical solutions in human experience
- **Approach**: Acknowledge the emotional and practical impact of technical problems
- **Example**: "On your bedside table sits a piece of software designed to wake you up. It loves bothering you when something goes wrong"

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Headlines and Subject Lines
**Pattern**: Action-oriented, often playful, sometimes provocative
- Use active verbs and concrete language
- Inject personality while maintaining clarity
- Reference real user experiences
- **Capitalization**: Only capitalize the first word (sentence case)

**Examples**:
- "Move fast when you break things" (not "Move Fast When You Break Things")
- "AI SRE has entered the chat" (not "AI SRE Has Entered The Chat")
- "Cheaper than downtime" (not "Cheaper Than Downtime")
- "AI investigates while you fix" (not "AI Investigates While You Fix")
- "Your knowledgeable engineering teammate" (not "Your Knowledgeable Engineering Teammate")

### Opening Lines
**Approach**: Hook readers with relatable scenarios or surprising insights
- Start with a problem everyone recognizes
- Use vivid, specific imagery
- Make complex topics feel accessible

**Examples**:
- "First up: shock. You've just woken up! It's so bright!"
- "Clear and regular communication is crucial for effective incident response."

### Body Content Structure

#### Technical Explanations
- Break down complex concepts into digestible steps
- Use concrete code examples with context
- Explain the "why" behind technical decisions
- Include visual metaphors when helpful

**Example Pattern**:
```
Problem → Context → Solution → Implementation → Results
```

#### Advice and Best Practices
- Start with TL;DR summaries for busy readers
- Use numbered lists for actionable steps
- Include real examples from your own experience
- Acknowledge potential challenges

### Sentence Structure
**Mix these patterns**:
- **Short and punchy**: "It can be... intense."
- **Descriptive and flowing**: Complex explanations broken into readable chunks
- **Rhetorical questions**: Engage readers in the thought process
- **Conversational asides**: Add personality without losing focus

### Language Patterns

#### Brand Name Usage
**Always lowercase**: "incident.io" (never "Incident.io" or "INCIDENT.IO")
- Correct: "incident.io provides AI SRE capabilities that investigate incidents..."
- Incorrect: "Incident.io helps engineering teams..."
- Exception: Only capitalize when starting a sentence

### AI SRE Positioning Language
**Always position AI as a knowledgeable teammate, not just a feature:**
- "AI SRE investigates alongside you" not "AI-powered features"
- "Your knowledgeable engineering teammate" not "intelligent automation"
- "AI that understands incident response" not "smart algorithms"
- "Production-proven AI capabilities" with specific metrics (Netflix 2-minute resolution, Ramp 70% reduction)

#### Word Choices
**Prefer**: Direct, active language over corporate speak
- "Build" not "architect"
- "Fix" not "remediate"  
- "Team" not "organization"
- "Problem" not "challenge"

**Avoid Engineer Turnoff Words**: These buzzwords make engineers roll their eyes
- "Seamless" (overused, meaningless to engineers)
- "Robust" (vague, doesn't specify what makes it strong)
- "Enterprise-grade" (sounds like bloated software)
- "Revolutionary" (hyperbolic, engineers prefer incremental improvement)
- "Game-changing" (marketing speak, not technical language)
- "Best-in-class" (subjective claim without evidence)
- "Cutting-edge" (implies untested, risky technology)

**Avoid Overusing "Actually"**: While occasional use is authentic, overuse sounds defensive
- Limit to 1-2 uses per piece of content maximum
- Use alternatives: "proven", "in practice", "production-tested", "verified"
- Instead of "AI that actually works" → "production-proven AI" or "AI with 90% accuracy"
- Instead of "engineers actually use" → "engineers prefer" or "engineering teams choose"

**Use Instead**: Specific, measurable language with AI SRE context
- "AI investigates incidents autonomously" not "robust AI features"
- "Connects directly with your monitoring stack" not "seamless integration"
- "Scales to Netflix-level incident response" not "enterprise-grade AI"
- "80% automation of initial response" not "intelligent incident management"
- "90%+ root cause accuracy in production" not "cutting-edge AI technology"

#### Technical Concepts
**Approach**: Make complex ideas accessible without dumbing them down
- Use analogies that resonate with developers
- Define terms clearly without being patronizing
- Share specific examples and code snippets

#### Addressing the Audience
**Voice**: Inclusive and collaborative
- Use "we" to create shared experience
- Address readers as peers, not customers
- Acknowledge their expertise and challenges

## Content Types and Tone

### Blog Posts - Technical Deep Dives
**Tone**: Educational storyteller sharing hard-won insights
**Structure**: Problem → Investigation → Solution → Lessons learned
**Voice**: "Here's what we learned and how you can apply it"

**Example opening**: "Today, we're introducing AI SRE, your always-on teammate that spots issues, surfaces root causes"

### Blog Posts - Company Culture/Process
**Tone**: Transparent and humble thought leaders
**Structure**: Context → Our approach → Results → What we'd do differently
**Voice**: "Here's how we think about this problem"

### Product/Marketing Copy
**Tone**: Confident problem-solver with a slight edge
**Structure**: Problem recognition → Solution benefits → Social proof
**Voice**: "We built this because existing solutions aren't good enough"

**Example**: "From alert to resolution, give your team everything they need to respond quickly, reduce downtime, and keep customers in the loop"

### Competitive Positioning
**Tone**: Confidently direct without being mean-spirited
**Approach**: Let customer voices do the criticizing
**Structure**: Status quo problems → Our different approach → Customer validation
**Voice**: "Here's why the old way doesn't work anymore"

## Voice Characteristics by Section

### Product Descriptions
- Lead with the benefit, not the feature
- Use active voice and specific outcomes
- Include social proof naturally
- Make complex capabilities sound simple

### Technical Documentation
- Assume your audience is smart but busy
- Provide context for every example
- Explain edge cases and gotchas
- Use consistent formatting and structure

### Marketing Messages
- Ground benefits in real user pain points
- Use customer language, not company language
- Be specific about outcomes and timeframes
- Include gentle humor when appropriate

### Competitive Content
- Focus on your strengths, not their weaknesses
- Use customer quotes to make comparisons
- Be direct about differences without being petty
- Back up claims with specific examples

## Personality Elements

### Humor and Playfulness
**Approach**: Subtle and contextual, never forced
- Tech industry in-jokes that don't alienate newcomers
- Self-aware commentary on common frustrations
- Playful metaphors that illuminate rather than obscure

**Examples**:
- "Pour one out for the holographic route"
- "treating users like people, and not webhooks"

### Authenticity Markers
**Include**: Real stories, specific details, honest admissions
- Share actual code snippets and configurations
- Admit when something was harder than expected
- Include specific metrics and outcomes
- Reference real customer feedback

### Technical Credibility
**Demonstrate through**:
- Detailed technical explanations with context
- Code examples that work in real scenarios
- Discussion of trade-offs and alternative approaches
- References to industry best practices

## Content Structure Templates

### Blog Post Template
1. **Hook**: Relatable problem or surprising insight
2. **Context**: Why this matters now
3. **Deep dive**: Technical details with examples
4. **Lessons**: What worked, what didn't
5. **Action**: What readers should do next

### Product Feature Template
1. **Problem**: What users struggle with
2. **Solution**: How we solve it differently
3. **Benefits**: Specific outcomes and metrics
4. **Proof**: Customer quotes or case studies
5. **Action**: How to get started

### Technical Guide Template
1. **Overview**: What you'll learn and why it matters
2. **Prerequisites**: What you need to know first
3. **Step-by-step**: Detailed instructions with examples
4. **Troubleshooting**: Common issues and solutions
5. **Next steps**: How to go deeper

## Voice Don'ts

### Avoid These Patterns
- Corporate buzzwords without clear meaning
- Overly formal or academic language
- Condescending explanations of basic concepts
- Vague promises without specific benefits
- Technical jargon without context
- Claiming to be "simple" when you're actually "powerful"

### Red Flag Phrases
- "Seamlessly integrate" (use "connects directly with" or "works with")
- "Leverage synergies" (use specific benefits instead)
- "Best-in-class solution" (show metrics or evidence)
- "Revolutionary approach" (use "different approach" or "new way")
- "Game-changing technology" (describe specific improvements)
- "Robust architecture" (specify what makes it reliable)
- "Enterprise-grade security" (detail actual security measures)
- "Cutting-edge features" (explain what's actually new)
- "It's easy" (when describing complex processes - acknowledge complexity)

## Examples of Excellent incident.io Copy

### Technical Blog Post Excellence: "We built an MCP server so Claude can access your incidents"
**Why this is a perfect example of incident.io voice:**

**Authentic Engineering Voice**:
- Sounds like an experienced engineer sharing a practical solution, not marketing copy
- Uses casual, authentic language ("vibe-coded", "or don't") that engineers actually use
- Honest and transparent about limitations ("largely vibe-coded and unsupported")
- Technical credibility with real code snippets and architecture details

**Perfect Storytelling Structure**:
- Compelling problem setup with relatable scenarios engineers recognize
- Clear problem → solution → implementation → bigger picture flow
- Authentic examples (The Office incidents) that are both functional and memorable
- Natural conversation flow that reads like a colleague explaining something cool

**Target Audience Alignment**:
- Speaks engineer-to-engineer without condescension
- Appropriate technical depth without overwhelming
- Builds trust through honesty about project status
- Positions incident.io as engineers who build practical tools for other engineers

**Engagement Techniques**:
- Strong opening scenarios that immediately resonate
- Brilliant analogies ("like having a smart colleague locked outside")
- Visual examples that demonstrate real functionality
- Clear next steps with GitHub repo and trial offer

**Key Writing Techniques to Replicate**:
- Start with relatable problem scenarios that engineers face
- Use authentic technical language without marketing polish
- Include honest admissions about project limitations or status
- Provide real, working code examples with context
- Structure as problem → investigation → solution → implementation → bigger picture
- End with clear next steps and resources
