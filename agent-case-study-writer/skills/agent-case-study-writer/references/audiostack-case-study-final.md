# AudioStack.ai Case Study

## How AudioStack.ai went from knowledge silos to democratized incident response with incident.io

An AI audio production platform serving Fortune 500 enterprises replaced PagerDuty's fragmented tooling with incident.io, enabling junior engineers to independently resolve complex incidents while giving their commercial team real-time visibility into system reliability.

---

## The Company

AudioStack.ai is revolutionizing audio production with their AI-native platform that generates 10,000-30,000 audio assets monthly for enterprise customers. As a Series A startup with a European engineering team serving global markets from Singapore to Silicon Valley, they're helping Fortune 500 companies in media, entertainment, and advertising transform how they create audio at scale.

**Industry:** AI/Audio Technology  
**Team Size:** Series A startup, distributed engineering team  
**Location:** Europe (serving global customers)  
**Use Case:** Democratizing incident response across engineering levels

---

## The Challenge

### Knowledge silos created dangerous retention risk

When AudioStack.ai started winning Fortune 500 clients, their incident management approach became a critical liability. Only one or two senior engineers could handle most incidents, creating an unsustainable bottleneck.

"You don't want to put all the load on one or two engineers. Because then you have a retention risk," explains Peadar Coyle, CTO and Co-founder. The concentration of incident response knowledge wasn't just inefficient—it was an existential risk to their enterprise relationships.

### Fragmented tools frustrated everyone

The team was drowning in tool sprawl. PagerDuty handled alerting (with what Coyle describes as a "woeful user experience"), Notion stored runbooks that nobody could find during incidents, and a separate status page required manual updates. Critical information lived in three different places, making effective incident response nearly impossible.

### Commercial teams operated blind

The most painful gap was communication. AudioStack.ai's commercial team—the people actually talking to enterprise customers—had zero visibility into incidents. They couldn't answer basic questions about system status or provide meaningful updates to clients who expected transparency.

"When you sell to the Enterprise. They don't want to accept any. They're very concerned," Coyle notes. Enterprise customers demand reliability and transparency, but AudioStack.ai's tools made both impossible.

---

## The Solution

### Two weeks that changed everything

AudioStack.ai's evaluation was brutally practical. During their two-week trial, three real incidents struck—perfect timing for an authentic test. No carefully orchestrated demos, just real production issues that needed solving.

The third incident became their "aha" moment. "One of them, the more junior engineers, was able to resolve the third incident because he just looked at the previous incidents and then noticed there was a pattern," Coyle recalls. A junior engineer had just solved a complex issue independently—something that would have been impossible with their old setup.

### Meeting engineers where they already work

The Slack-native approach resonated immediately. "There's something with that slack workflow. So you got to realize that the average engineer just spends a lot of their time in slack," Coyle observes. 

For a distributed team spanning European time zones and serving global customers, Slack was already their collaboration hub. incident.io didn't force them to add another dashboard to monitor—it brought incident management directly into their existing workflow.

### From trial to transformation in 6 weeks

The rollout was deliberately measured but swift:

- **Weeks 1-2:** Trial period with those three revealing incidents
- **Weeks 3-4:** Early adopters among engineering started using it
- **Weeks 5-6:** Parallel running with PagerDuty as a safety net
- **Week 6:** Full migration including commercial team integration

"We rolled it out first with some of the keener engineers, and then we actually rolled it out to the commercial team as well," Coyle explains. The gradual approach built confidence while maintaining operational safety.

## The Results

### From reactive to proactive incident culture

The transformation was immediate and dramatic. Junior engineers went from helpless observers to confident responders. That pattern-recognition incident during the trial—where a junior engineer independently resolved a complex issue by spotting patterns in previous incidents—wasn't a fluke. It became the new normal.

"You don't want to put all the load on one or two engineers. Because then you have a retention risk," Coyle had worried. Now, knowledge that was previously locked in senior engineers' heads was accessible to everyone through searchable incident history and automated runbooks. Any engineer could confidently take on-call.

The impact rippled beyond engineering. "The customer facing team loved it. Because they could get the information from Slack themselves," Coyle emphasizes. No more frantic messages asking engineering for updates. No more embarrassing "let me check and get back to you" responses to enterprise clients.

The transformation was so complete that during a sprint demo, a commercial team member independently presented incident.io metrics and reliability data. "That was also a kind of cool moment where I was like, 'Oh, that's really cool. A commercial person is talking about incident.io,'" Coyle recalls.

The success wasn't accidental—it stemmed from fundamental design decisions that aligned perfectly with AudioStack.ai's needs.

**Consolidation over sprawl.** Where they previously juggled PagerDuty, Notion, and a separate status page, now everything lived in one place. "PagerDuty has a woeful user experience," Coyle states bluntly. The consolidation wasn't just about efficiency—when incidents strike, teams no longer waste precious minutes hunting for information across platforms.

**Slack-first, not Slack-maybe.** "There's something with that slack workflow. So you got to realize that the average engineer just spends a lot of their time in slack," Coyle observes. This isn't a web app with Slack notifications bolted on. It's incident management that lives entirely where AudioStack.ai's distributed team already collaborates 24/7.

**Knowledge democratization by design.** The platform doesn't just document incidents—it makes that knowledge immediately actionable. Junior engineers can learn from past incidents, spot patterns, and resolve issues that would have previously escalated. This wasn't a happy accident but a core design principle that transformed their incident culture.

**Startup-friendly reality.** Unlike enterprise tools that assume unlimited budgets and dedicated incident commanders, incident.io understood AudioStack.ai's reality: a lean team where everyone wears multiple hats. The pricing and functionality reflected this understanding.

**Support that gets it.** When AudioStack.ai needed help, they didn't get generic enterprise support scripts. They got people who understood startup constraints and provided practical solutions that worked within their reality.
