## Case Study: AudioStack.ai

### How AudioStack.ai democratized incident response and earned enterprise trust with incident.io

**An AI audio platform that produces 10,000+ assets monthly for Fortune 500 enterprises replaced PagerDuty's "woeful user experience" with incident.io, enabling junior engineers to resolve complex incidents and giving commercial teams instant visibility into system reliability.**

### Company Profile

**AudioStack.ai:** Series A AI-native platform revolutionizing audio production at scale

Founded to transform how enterprises create audio content, AudioStack.ai represents the cutting edge of AI-powered creative production. Their platform enables brands to generate thousands of audio variations—from advertising spots to podcast content—in the time it would traditionally take to produce a single asset. This capability has attracted major Fortune 500 companies who need to personalize audio content at unprecedented scale.

- **Scale:** 10,000-30,000 audio assets monthly for retail customers, with individual campaigns sometimes requiring hundreds of variations
- **Market:** Media/entertainment, advertising agencies, podcast production, e-learning platforms, and retail brands
- **Team:** European engineering base serving global enterprise customers across Singapore, Australia, US, Canada, and beyond
- **Technology:** Proprietary AI models for voice synthesis, audio processing, and dynamic content generation
- **Leadership:** Peadar Coyle, CTO and Co-founder, bringing deep technical expertise in AI and distributed systems

### The Challenge: Growing pains that come with rapid scale

As AudioStack.ai evolved from ambitious startup to Series A company serving Fortune 500 clients, their incident management approach needed to mature alongside their technology. What worked for a small team of engineers who could coordinate over coffee no longer fit an organization with global customers expecting enterprise-grade reliability.

Enterprise customers weren't just buying AI audio technology—they were integrating AudioStack.ai into their production pipelines, marketing campaigns, and customer experiences. A single incident could impact thousands of audio assets across multiple campaigns.

"Our commercial team understood that being a startup, we've got to earn the trust of these enterprise customers. And one of the big things is how you handle incidents," explains Peadar Coyle, CTO and Co-founder.

The engineering team found themselves facing three interconnected challenges that many scaling startups will recognize:

**1. Knowledge concentration created organizational vulnerability**

Like many growing companies, AudioStack.ai had naturally developed specialists—engineers who knew specific systems inside and out. While this expertise was valuable, it created an uncomfortable reality: only one or two engineers could effectively handle most incidents. This knowledge concentration meant the team's incident response capability was highly dependent on specific individuals being available.

"If that person gets hit by a bus, then you're sort of fucked," Coyle notes with characteristic directness about the risk this posed.

Beyond the obvious operational risk, this dynamic created stress for those key engineers who felt the weight of being constantly needed, and frustration for other team members who wanted to contribute but lacked the context to help effectively.

**2. Tool sprawl complicated incident response**

The team's incident management toolkit had grown organically, resulting in a patchwork of solutions that made coordinated response challenging. They were juggling PagerDuty (which Coyle describes as having a "woeful user experience"), ad-hoc Notion runbooks that were helpful but disconnected, and a separate status page that required manual updates.

Each tool served a purpose, but critical incident information was scattered across multiple platforms. During an actual incident—when speed and clarity matter most—engineers had to jump between different interfaces, remember multiple logins, and manually sync information between systems. This fragmentation made it difficult to maintain a clear picture of what was happening, who was doing what, and what customers were being told.

**3. Information silos left commercial teams unable to support customers**

Perhaps most frustrating was the disconnect between engineering and commercial teams. The customer-facing staff—those fielding anxious calls from enterprise clients—had minimal visibility into incidents affecting their accounts. They couldn't answer basic questions about system status, provide meaningful timeline estimates, or proactively communicate with customers about issues.

This wasn't just inconvenient; it was damaging to customer relationships. Enterprise clients expected transparency and proactive communication, especially when their own operations might be affected. The commercial team wanted to provide this level of service but lacked the tools and access to do so effectively.

### The Evaluation: Real incidents provided the perfect test environment

AudioStack.ai's approach to evaluating incident.io was refreshingly practical. Rather than relying on demos and hypothetical scenarios, they initiated a two-week trial that coincidentally—and fortuitously—included three real incidents.

"We had some incidents during the trial period. So that was actually quite useful for us to evaluate," Coyle recalls with the hindsight that these incidents, while stressful at the time, provided invaluable validation.

The evaluation process wasn't just about features and functionality. The team needed to see if incident.io could address their specific challenges: Could it help junior engineers contribute meaningfully? Would it actually consolidate their fragmented tooling? Could non-technical team members use it effectively?

The third incident during the trial became a pivotal moment that answered these questions decisively: "One of them, the more junior engineers, was able to resolve the third incident because he just looked at the previous incidents and then noticed there was a pattern."

This wasn't a carefully orchestrated demo or a contrived test scenario—it was a junior engineer, in the middle of a real incident affecting real customers, successfully identifying and resolving an issue by learning from documented history. The platform had already begun delivering on its promise of democratizing incident response.

### The Implementation: A measured rollout that built confidence

AudioStack.ai's implementation strategy reflected the thoughtful approach of a team that understood change management. Rather than forcing a sudden switch, they designed a phased rollout that allowed different groups to adopt the platform at a comfortable pace while maintaining operational safety.

**The 8-week journey from trial to full adoption:**

**Weeks 1-2: Trial by fire**
The initial trial period with live incidents provided immediate validation. The team could see how incident.io handled real-world scenarios, not just demo environments. This hands-on experience built confidence among the engineering team who would be the primary users.

**Weeks 3-4: Early adopter phase**
"We rolled it out first with some of the keener engineers, and then we actually rolled it out to the commercial team as well," Coyle explains. Starting with enthusiastic early adopters created internal champions who could help others navigate the transition.

**Weeks 5-8: Parallel operations for safety**
The team maintained both PagerDuty and incident.io during this period, ensuring they had a fallback option if needed. This parallel running gave everyone—from senior engineers to new hires—time to familiarize themselves with the new workflows without the pressure of it being the only option.

**Week 8: Full commitment**
With confidence built through successful incident resolution and team-wide familiarity, AudioStack.ai fully migrated to incident.io. Importantly, this included rolling out access to the commercial team, transforming incident management from an engineering-only concern to a company-wide capability.

The implementation wasn't just technical—it involved documentation updates, runbook migration, team training sessions, and establishing new communication protocols. The measured pace allowed these cultural and process changes to take root alongside the technology adoption.

### The Results: Transformation beyond the metrics

The impact of implementing incident.io went far beyond improved response times or reduced manual work. AudioStack.ai experienced a fundamental shift in how incidents were handled and who could handle them.

**Junior engineers stepped up as capable incident responders**

The most striking change was the democratization of incident response. Junior engineers who previously could only watch and learn during incidents became active participants and even incident commanders. They used incident history, runbooks, and accumulated knowledge to resolve issues independently.

"One of them, the more junior engineers, was able to resolve the third incident because he just looked at the previous incidents and then noticed there was a pattern," Coyle emphasizes. This single incident validated the entire platform migration—it proved that institutional knowledge could be captured, shared, and leveraged by any team member.

This shift had cascading benefits: Senior engineers felt less pressure being the only ones who could handle certain issues. Junior engineers gained confidence and developed skills faster. The entire team became more resilient, as incident response capability was no longer concentrated in a few individuals.

**Commercial team became true partners in incident management**

The transformation of the commercial team's role was equally significant. What had been a frustrating information vacuum became transparent, accessible insight into system status and incident progress.

"The customer facing team loved it. Because they could get the information from Slack themselves, or they could just click on the link page," Coyle reports. 

This wasn't just about access to information—it was about empowerment. Commercial team members could now proactively communicate with customers, set accurate expectations, and even contribute to incident resolution by providing customer context and impact assessment.

The cultural shift was crystallized during a sprint demo when something unexpected happened: "That was also a kind of cool moment where I was like, 'Oh, that's really cool. A commercial person is talking about incident.io.'"

A member of the commercial team had independently pulled incident.io metrics and presented them during the demo, showing how incident patterns correlated with customer feedback. This moment represented a fundamental change—incident management had become a shared responsibility and source of insight across the organization.

**Slack transformed from chat tool to command center**

"There's something with that slack workflow. So you got to realize that the average engineer just spends a lot of their time in slack," Coyle observes. By meeting engineers where they already work, incident.io eliminated the context switching that had made their previous setup so frustrating.

But this went beyond mere convenience. Slack channels became living documentation of incident response, accessible to anyone who needed context. The integration meant that incident management became part of the natural flow of work rather than a disruptive context switch to separate tools.

### The Engineering Reality: Why Slack-native matters more than you think

Coyle's perspective on the Slack integration reveals a fundamental truth about modern engineering workflows that vendors often miss:

"The average engineer just spends a lot of their time in slack. I mean, my anecdotal evidence, we're a remote company, we're European engineers serving American companies basically, or global companies from Singapore, Australia, etc. These engineers, they communicate in Slack all the time."

This observation goes deeper than tool preference. For distributed teams—which AudioStack.ai epitomizes with their European engineers serving global customers—Slack isn't just a communication tool. It's the virtual office, the water cooler, the war room, and the knowledge base all rolled into one.

When an incident strikes at 3 AM, the last thing an engineer wants is to remember which tool to open, which login to use, or where that runbook was stored. They're already in Slack, probably already discussing the issue with teammates. Having incident management happen in that same context removes friction at the moment when every second counts.

For AudioStack.ai, this meant that adoption was natural rather than forced. Engineers didn't have to learn a new tool—they just had new capabilities within their existing workflow. This seamless integration was particularly important for junior engineers who might feel intimidated by complex incident management platforms but were completely comfortable in Slack.
